Base

The base of an exponent is the number being multiplied by itself. In the expression $b^n$, $b$ would be the only base. It is understood that $b$ is multiplied by one. However, in the following expression, $ab^n$ (where $a\neq1$), $a$ and $b$ are the bases. This leads us to understand that the number of bases is equivalent to the number of terms before the exponent.

For example, take the following expressions:

  • $-7n^3$
  • $-3n^2$

The bases of the first expression are $-7$ and $n$, and the bases of the second expression are $-3$ and $n$.

Exponent

The exponent is the number written in the subscript; it is the number of times you will multiply the base by itself. In the expression $a^n$, $n$ is the exponent; this is "shorthand" for writing $a x a (x a (x a...))$ $n$ times. With this, we know that if $n\eq1$, then $a$ is itself. All numbers without an explicitly stated exponent are implied to be raised to the power of one.

Of course, there is the oddity of raising a base to the power of zero.

Take the following expressions:

  • $-7n^2$
  • $9x$

The first expression is equivalent to writing $7^1\times\left(n^1\timesn^1\right)$, and the second expression is equivalent to $9^1\timesn^1$, but is left better simplified as $9x$.
