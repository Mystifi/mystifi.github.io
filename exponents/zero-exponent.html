Recall from the previous page that, in the expression \(b^n\), \(n\) is the exponent, or the number of times that \(b\) is multiplied by itself. We discussed how anything raised to the power of one was itself; if it were raised to the power of two (commonly referred to as squared), then it would be \(n\cdot n\), et cetera.

However, if \(n\eq 0\), \(b\eq 1\). Let's break it down a little further.

If you've already read Multiplying and Dividing Exponents, Same Base, then you're already familiar with the concept of dividing exponents when they share the same base. If you haven't, then the formula generally looks like this. $$\frac{a^b}{a^c}$$

Following the rules of dividing exponents with the same base, you simply subtract the bottom exponent \(c\) from the top exponent, \(b\). Thus, we can infer: $$\frac{a^b}{a^c}\eq a^{b - c}$$

Commonly, we know this makes sense. Given that any number over itself is 1, we can represent this as \(\frac{a}{a}\eq 1\). Since any number without any explicit exponents is implicitly raised to the power of one, we can rewrite this to be \(\frac{a^1}{a^1}\eq 1\), which, according to the previous paragraph, is equal to the following equation: \(\frac{a^1}{a^1}\eq a^{1-1}\eq a^0\eq 1\). This shows that a number raised to the power of zero is equivalent to one.

Let's apply this to the following expression. $$\frac{-6x^2y}{6x^2y}$$ Let's rewrite our expression to include all instances of numbers being implicitly raised to the power of one: $$\frac{-6^1x^2y^1}{6^1x^2y^1}$$ Now, we can simply subtract each exponent from the top to the bottom: $$-\frac{6^1x^2y^1}{6^1x^2y^1}\eq -(6^{1-1}x^{2-2}y^{1-1})$$ (I simply moved the negative one to the outside of the fraction from the numerator.) After simplification: $$-\frac{6^1x^2y^1}{6^1x^2y^1}\eq -(6^0x^0y^0)\eq -(1\cdot 1\cdot 1)\eq -1$$
